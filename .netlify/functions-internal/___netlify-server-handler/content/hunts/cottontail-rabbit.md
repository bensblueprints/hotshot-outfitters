## Why Cottontail Rabbit Hunting in Michigan's Thumb Is Underrated

Most hunters driving through Huron County see the agricultural fields and think deer. Fair enough. But the brushy fencerows, overgrown ditches, multiflora rose thickets, and weedy field edges that border all those cornfields and soybean fields? That's prime cottontail habitat, and it holds rabbits in numbers that would surprise most hunters who've never worked it.

Cottontail rabbits thrive in exactly the kind of edge cover that dominates this region: brushy tangles near open feeding areas, dense grass and shrub patches along drainage ditches, and the scrubby second-growth that fills in along fence lines and woodlot edges. This is hunter-on-foot country. You push through cover, the rabbit bolts, and you've got about two seconds to make your shot before it disappears into the next thicket. It's fast, physical, and addictive.

Michigan hunting doesn't get more accessible than this. You don't need a treestand or a months-long scouting mission. You need good boots, a shotgun or .22, and the willingness to push cover until the rabbits start moving.

## Michigan Cottontail Rabbit Season

Michigan DNR establishes the cottontail rabbit season annually. As a general planning reference:

- **Season**: Typically opens September 15 and runs through March 31
- **Daily Bag Limit**: Check current MI DNR regulations — limits apply
- **Possession Limit**: Twice the daily bag limit in most cases

The September through October window gives you warm-weather hunting in dense green cover — tough going, but rabbits are concentrated in predictable areas near food. November and December hunting after a frost or early snowfall is often the most productive: cover thins out, rabbit tracks are visible in snow, and the animals become more predictable in their movements. February and March offer late-season hunting when rabbits are on late-winter food sources and pressure has dropped off.

Always verify the current season dates and bag limits at the [MI DNR website](https://www.michigan.gov/dnr) before your trip. Regulations can shift between years.

## Terrain: What the Hunting Ground Looks Like

Huron County's rabbit hunting terrain is a mix of agricultural edge cover and woodlot periphery. The specific types of cover we work include:

**Fencerow tangles**: Old hedgerows and fence lines that have grown up into dense multiflora rose, Russian olive, and native shrubs. Rabbits use these as travel corridors and bedding areas throughout the season.

**Drainage ditches**: Overgrown ditches along field edges hold cattail stands, tall grass, and brushy cover that rabbits use for security. Working these linear features produces consistent flushes.

**Woodlot edges**: The transition from open field to timber often includes a brushy fringe of regenerating shrubs and saplings. This is classic cottontail bedding habitat, especially on south-facing edges that capture winter sun.

**Overgrown fields**: Older fields returning to brush, with tall grass and scattered shrub cover, are rabbit magnets. This type of early successional habitat is exactly what cottontails prefer.

## Licensing and What You Need

Every hunter on a guided rabbit hunt in Michigan must hold:

- **Valid Michigan Hunting License** (base license, which covers small game including rabbits)
- Non-residents must purchase a non-resident license

Michigan's small game license is straightforward and relatively affordable. Purchase it online through the MI DNR portal or at any authorized license agent in the state. Rabbits do not require a separate stamp or tag beyond the base small game license.

Confirm current licensing requirements with the MI DNR before your trip. We will answer licensing questions during the booking process, but the responsibility for legal licensing rests with each hunter.

## Gear Notes for Michigan Rabbit Hunting

Rabbit hunting is gear-simple but clothing-demanding. The cover you'll be pushing through will destroy ordinary pants in a single morning. Invest in briar-proof or upland hunting pants — Filson, Carhartt, or any purpose-built upland brush pant will save your legs. Same goes for your jacket if you're pushing dense rose thickets.

Footwear matters. You'll be walking for hours on uneven agricultural ground, through wet drainage ditches and across plowed field edges. Waterproof boots with ankle support keep you comfortable and mobile. Rubber boots work well for wet early-season hunting; insulated leather hunting boots work better for cold late-season conditions.

For shotgunners, open chokes perform best in close-cover situations. Improved cylinder or modified choke gives you the spread you need at typical rabbit-hunting distances. If you're shooting tight cover where shots are measured in yards, improved cylinder is the right call.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is cottontail rabbit hunting good for beginners or kids?**

It's one of the best hunts for new hunters. Action is frequent, the equipment requirements are simple, and the physical demands are manageable for most fitness levels. It's also a relatively affordable guided experience. If you're introducing someone to hunting, a rabbit hunt is an excellent choice.

**Q: What shotgun loads work best for cottontail rabbits?**

For 12-gauge, #6 or #7.5 lead shot in a 2.75" or 3" shell is the standard. For 20-gauge, same shot sizes work well. Open chokes — improved cylinder or modified — are preferred for the close-range shots you'll typically take in heavy cover. Steel shot is not required for cottontails.

**Q: Can I bring my own beagle?**

Possibly. We accommodate hunters who want to run their own dogs on a case-by-case basis depending on property considerations and group dynamics. Discuss this during the booking process and we'll tell you what's feasible.

**Q: Is blaze orange required for rabbit hunting in Michigan?**

During concurrent firearm deer season (November 15–30 and potentially other firearm seasons), Michigan law requires hunters in the field to wear blaze orange. Check current MI DNR regulations for the specific season you're hunting. We will keep you informed of requirements applicable to your hunt date.

**Q: How physically demanding is a guided rabbit hunt?**

You should expect to walk several miles over the course of a half-day or full-day hunt, through varied and sometimes rough terrain. The cover can be dense and physically challenging to push through. Most reasonably fit hunters handle it without issue, but if you have mobility concerns, discuss them with us at booking and we'll plan accordingly.

**Q: Do you offer half-day rabbit hunting options?**

Yes. Rabbit hunts can be structured as half-day or full-day experiences. Contact us to discuss the format that fits your schedule and group.
