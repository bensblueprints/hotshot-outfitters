## Michigan Crow Season — Dates and Legal Framework

Crows are classified as migratory game birds under the Federal Migratory Bird Treaty Act, which means their seasons are set cooperatively between the U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service and individual states. Michigan has established crow hunting seasons that provide generous windows for hunting while protecting nesting periods.

Michigan's current crow hunting framework includes open seasons generally in two windows:

- **August 1 through September 30**
- **February 1 through March 31**

Outside of those formal season windows, crows may not be taken under hunting regulations. However, during those open periods, there are no bag limits on crows in Michigan — hunting is seven days a week during open season.

It's also worth noting that Michigan law allows for the taking of crows under depredation provisions when they are actively destroying crops or threatening agricultural operations, outside of hunting season — but this is a separate legal framework from sport hunting and requires specific conditions to be met. For our guided hunts, we operate within the established Michigan open crow hunting seasons.

**Always verify current season dates and regulations at the [MI DNR website](https://www.michigan.gov/dnr) before your hunt.** Federal and state crow regulations can change, and it's the hunter's responsibility to know current rules.

## What to Expect on a Guided Crow Hunt

Crow hunting is a team sport. It works best with multiple shooters positioned in tight concealment, calling together or in sequence, and shooting quickly when crows commit to the spread. Here's how Hotshot Outfitters runs a crow hunt:

**Setup**: Your guide will scout the area prior to your hunt, locating active crow travel routes and feeding areas. We'll establish a hide — typically natural vegetation, brush, or pop-up blinds — near the decoy spread with clear shooting lanes in multiple directions.

**Calling**: Crow calling is an art. Electronic callers are legal in Michigan for crow hunting and are effective for producing the distress, assembly, and owl-alarm calls that pull crows from a distance. Mouth calls add realism in close. Your guide will manage the calling sequence to keep birds committed.

**Decoys**: A combination of full-body crow decoys and an owl decoy (crows harass owls instinctively — it's one of the most reliable calls you can run) set up on fence posts, brush piles, and the ground produces realistic presentations. We'll set the spread for the wind conditions and approach angles on your hunt day.

**The Shooting**: When it works, crows will come in fast and in numbers. You must stay concealed until the guide calls the shot, then shoot quickly and decisively. Crows that flare and survive will not come back. Coordination among hunters matters — set expectations before the birds arrive.

## Gear Notes for Crow Hunting

Concealment is the single most important gear consideration for crow hunting. These birds will identify and flare from a hunter at distances that would make most hunters skeptical. Full camouflage — face, hands, and all exposed skin — is baseline. Pop-up blinds are effective when natural vegetation is insufficient. Your gun barrel should be covered or have a matte finish. Glare from a blued barrel or scope has killed more crow setups than bad calling.

For shotguns, an open choke gives you better patterns at the typical 15–35 yard range where crows commit. Improved cylinder or modified is the call. If you're shooting birds at longer range during early passes, tighten up — but most killing shots happen inside 40 yards when birds are called properly.

Ear protection is worth mentioning because the shooting volume in a good crow hunt can be high. Bring electronic ear protection that lets you hear calling and guide communication while protecting your hearing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: When is the best time to go crow hunting in Michigan?**

August and early September, when crops are in the fields and crow populations are at summer highs, can produce huge numbers of birds. The February–March window is also productive, particularly after winter has pushed crows into tight feeding patterns. Both seasons have their character. August is warmer and birds are abundant; February is cold but crow behavior becomes predictable around food sources.

**Q: Are there bag limits on crows in Michigan?**

Currently, there are no bag limits on crows during the established open Michigan crow hunting seasons. However, regulations can change — always verify with the MI DNR before your hunt. Bag limits can be amended seasonally.

**Q: Can I use my regular hunting license for crow hunting?**

Michigan hunting license requirements for migratory birds can involve federal stamps and state licenses depending on the season and species. Verify the current licensing requirements with the MI DNR. Your guide will brief you on what's required for your specific hunt.

**Q: What's the best calling strategy for Michigan crows?**

A combination of assembly calls, distress calls, and owl alarm calls tends to produce the most consistent results. Electronic callers allow you to run multiple calls in sequence, which is more realistic than a single call repeated. Your guide will manage the calling — your job is to stay concealed and be ready to shoot.

**Q: Is crow hunting legal year-round in Michigan?**

No. Michigan's crow hunting season is limited to two windows: August 1–September 30 and February 1–March 31. Outside those seasons, sport hunting for crows is prohibited under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act framework. Verify current season dates with the MI DNR each year.

**Q: How many shooters work best for a crow hunt?**

Two to four shooters is typically the sweet spot. With more than four hunters, concealment becomes harder and coordination breaks down. Two hunters working as a team, with one focused on left-to-right crossing shots and one on the approach angle, can be devastatingly efficient.
