## Why Lake Huron's Thumb Coast Is a Duck Hunting Destination

Duck hunters in Michigan tend to concentrate in the Saginaw Bay area, the Upper Peninsula, and the St. Clair Flats. Those are all excellent destinations. But the eastern Lake Huron coastline through Huron County is consistently overlooked, and that oversight works in our favor.

The geography here puts you in front of multiple duck hunting opportunities simultaneously. The Lake Huron shoreline provides access to diving duck flights — bluebills (scaup), buffleheads, redheads, ring-necks, canvasbacks, goldeneye, and long-tailed ducks — that move along the coast during the fall migration. Divers work different than puddlers: they raft offshore, respond to big decoy spreads with confidence lines, and when conditions push them inshore, the shooting can be explosive.

Inland from the shoreline, the agricultural fields and pothole wetlands of Huron County hold resident and migrating puddle ducks — mallards, teal, wood ducks, black ducks, and wigeon. These birds work the grain fields and flooded corn edges, and hunting them requires a completely different approach: smaller decoy spreads, tighter concealment, and an understanding of feeding patterns.

The marsh and wetland fringe along shoreline drainages adds a third dimension: brushy creek mouths, cattail stands, and emergent wetland edges that hold mallards, teal, and black ducks in numbers that would surprise hunters unfamiliar with this coast.

## Michigan Duck Season Dates and Framework

Duck seasons in Michigan are set cooperatively by the U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service and the MI DNR under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act framework. Season dates, species-specific bag limits, and regulations are established annually — dates shift year to year based on population assessments.

As a general planning reference for Michigan:

- **Regular Duck Season**: Typically opens in early to mid-October and runs through late November or early December, depending on the zone (Michigan splits into North, South, and Restricted zones)
- **Teal Season**: Often a separate early teal-only season in September
- **Daily Bag Limits**: Species-specific and set annually — typically a mixed bag limit of six ducks per day with species-specific sub-limits; always verify current limits before hunting
- **Non-Toxic Shot**: Required for all waterfowl hunting in Michigan (steel, bismuth, TSS, etc.)

Huron County falls under Michigan's specific waterfowl zone designations. The MI DNR publishes a comprehensive waterfowl guide annually that covers zone boundaries, open dates, and bag limits.

**Always verify current season dates and regulations at the [MI DNR website](https://www.michigan.gov/dnr) and the U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service migratory bird regulations before your hunt.** Waterfowl regulations are among the most complex in hunting, and they change every year. Your guide will be current on regulations for your hunt date, but you are responsible for knowing the rules.

## Diver Ducks vs. Puddle Ducks: Two Different Hunts

It's worth explaining the distinction because the hunts are genuinely different in character.

**Diver Hunting on Lake Huron**: Setting up points, jetties, or shore positions with large decoy spreads extended out into the lake. Divers raft in open water and fly with speed and confidence — they don't hover and drop like mallards; they come in low, fast, and committed. The shooting is challenging in a different way. When a raft of bluebills decides to work your spread on a gray October morning, the pace is relentless.

**Puddle Duck Hunting in Fields and Marshes**: Working cut corn, flooded fields, pothole marshes, and creek mouth locations for mallards, blacks, teal, and wood ducks. These hunts are about reading daily feeding patterns and being in the right place when the birds move in the morning. Mallards on a late October morning dropping into flooded corn with a northwest wind behind them is a Michigan duck hunting experience worth traveling for.

We guide both types of hunts. Tell us what you're after and we'll put together the right hunt for you.

## Licensing and Federal Duck Stamp Requirements

Every waterfowl hunter in Michigan must hold:

- **Valid Michigan Hunting License** with a **Michigan Duck Stamp** (waterfowl base license requirement)
- **Federal Duck Stamp** — required for all waterfowl hunters 16 and older; available at USPS offices, license agents, and online at the USFWS website
- Non-residents must purchase appropriate non-resident Michigan licenses

Waterfowl hunting requires more licensing than most hunting — both state and federal requirements apply. Do not arrive without both your Michigan hunting license/duck stamp and your federal duck stamp. There are no exceptions and no workarounds.

## Gear Notes for Michigan Duck Hunting

Lake Huron in October and November is not a comfortable place to be underdressed. Wind-driven rain, cold temperatures, and the spray off chop on the lake combine in ways that cut through lighter clothing fast. Waterproof bibs and a waterproof jacket with seam sealing are not optional. Neoprene waders or quality breathable waders with appropriate insulation underneath are the baseline for marsh and shoreline setups.

For shotgun loads, Michigan waterfowl require non-toxic shot. Steel is the most common choice. For diver ducks — which are dense-feathered birds typically taken at longer ranges — #2 or #3 steel in a 3" or 3.5" shell provides the pattern density needed. For close-range marsh mallards, #4 or #3 steel works well. Modern premium non-toxic loads (bismuth, HEAVYWEIGHT, TSS) perform closer to lead at lower shot sizes — if you're willing to invest in premium loads, they're worth it.

Modified or improved modified choke is the standard for steel shot in 12-gauge. Do not shoot steel through full choke — it will damage the barrel.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need a Federal Duck Stamp to hunt ducks in Michigan?**

Yes. All waterfowl hunters 16 and older must possess a valid Federal Migratory Bird Hunting and Conservation Stamp (Federal Duck Stamp) in addition to their state license. It is available online at the USFWS website, at USPS locations, and at many sporting goods stores.

**Q: What species of ducks are common on Lake Huron's Thumb coast?**

The coastal diver flight includes scaup (bluebills), buffleheads, ring-necked ducks, redheads, goldeneye, and long-tailed ducks (oldsquaw). Inland and marsh hunting adds mallards, black ducks, teal (early season), wood ducks, and wigeon to the mix. Species composition and abundance varies by year and weather conditions.

**Q: What kind of weather produces the best duck hunting in Michigan's Thumb?**

Cold fronts from the northwest produce the best duck movement, particularly for divers along the Lake Huron coast. A two- or three-day blow followed by clearing skies and northwest wind will push migrating birds and concentrate them along the shoreline. Overcast, calm mornings are often good for marsh and field hunting where calling keeps birds engaged.

**Q: Can I hunt ducks with lead shot in Michigan?**

No. Non-toxic shot is federally mandated for all waterfowl hunting in the United States. Steel shot is the standard; bismuth, TSS, and other approved non-toxic alternatives are legal but more expensive. Your guide will confirm what you need.

**Q: Do you guide early teal season hunts in Michigan?**

Ask us when you book. Early teal season is typically a short September window — dates vary by year. If teal season overlaps with our available guide schedule, we can discuss putting together a teal-specific hunt.

**Q: What is the daily bag limit for ducks in Michigan?**

Bag limits are set annually under the Migratory Bird Treaty framework and are species-specific. A combined daily bag limit of six ducks per day is the general federal framework, with sub-limits on specific species (canvasback, redhead, pintail, etc.). Verify current limits at the MI DNR and USFWS websites before your hunt — limits change year to year.
